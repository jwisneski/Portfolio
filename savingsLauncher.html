Tl;dr

In 2017 Amazon's one-click patent expired. We theorized more and more retailers would be using this to reduce the distance for customers to buy products. The biggest blocker for a one-click checkout at Kohl's is the use of discounts.

With my experience on previous discount projects, I took the lead on this one. We kicked off a series of bi-weekly tests to learn as much as we could about our customers and get quick feedback.

Today the first phase of The Savings Launcher is live at Kohl's and it resulted in sales of over $100 million in the first year.

It all started with a sketch

The first thing we did was break down the business's rules around discounts. Rather than putting the emphasis on the individual discounts we focused on the activity around the discounts. The customer could just save money, spend a little more to get better discounts later or save Kohl's Cash for another purchase later. These were all activities we'd observed from our customers before this project.

I shouldn't need a Ph.D. to check out

The first iteration was a bit of a misstep. We failed to realize that this doesn't resolve the core issue we were trying to solve for, the Kohl's discount model is complex.

This used to be a gamified way for customers to save money but in the years since its inception, competitors have only made their online checkout experience easier.

Built from the insights of the Mini Bag

We uncovered a number of insights during our testing. This was where our first doubts about the discount model started to pop up. It seemed our customers had grown tired of the discount model and lack of transparency in pricing.

The Savings Settings

Our first iteration used behaviors, rather than dollars or percentages, to drive the use of discounts. To entice our business partners we included scenarios to drive the use of Kohl's Charge Cards.

Focusing on new customers

When we started testing these concepts we realized our audience had to be new customers. These were customers Kohl's needed to attract and customers who refused to play the game of stacking discounts.

Customers don't want to work

We tested these concepts with new customers but we could't lose existing customers by changing the way discounts worked. We made sure to include existing customers in our testing.

Testing it

Every two weeks we put concepts in front of customers. Every two weeks we learned more and more about them. Our biggest suprise was that existing customers and new customers alike preferred our simplier discount interface.

Further simplifying the savings

We put our concepts in front of 26 customers over 3 months. With each round of testing we improved our product and updated our assumptions. We tried an onboarding process but realized we could design something much more engaging without it.

Inviting use

From the first interaction we made our experience a little cheeky. All 26 customer were unsure what a Savings Launcher is but all 26 wanted to find out without us directing them to it.

Multiple feedback mechanisms

We were very deliberate with how we showed the customer's discounts both with the stacking verbage and with the functional animations we used.

The new onboarding process

Rather than pause our customer's exploration to show them what the Savings Launcher is, we let them explore. This was a safe, easy way to test out different discounts. Customers can't use the wrong discounts and there are no error messages.

Autopilot

Early in the design process we realized there needed to be an "easy mode". To keep with the nature of design to this point we lableled it "Autopilot". When engaged it gives the customer the best deal given all of their possible discounts.

All 26 customers engaged Autopilot and compared the on and off states. 25 of those customers kept Autopilot on.

“I feel like Kohl's has my back.”

Trust

It was at this point that we realized the most important part about our customers. They expect Kohl's to act in their best interest because of their loyalty. Any business-centric thoughts of diluting Autopilot, withholding offers so not everyone gets the best deal, were immediately halted. This would break down the trust that so many Kohl's customers have.

The anatomy of an offer

Each discount has some basic information in common. In the marketing for offers these aren't always explained in the same way. We wanted to break from that tradition and add a layer of consistency to our offers.

Phase 1

The first phase of the project was released in April of 2017. While not all of the idea made it, we did make some big strides. Customers can enjoy a new level of transparency that never existed at Kohl's.

Connecting customers and products

This was the first step in creating a new, more competitive Kohl's. From checkout we started to work backwards and refine what a product page can be. I'm looking forward to seeing some of the work we started in the coming months and years.

Reflections on The Savings Launcher

This project was so much fun. Not only was it a chance to think broadly but we were really designing with customers, not just for them.

I appreciate that this was one of the more pure design processes. Largely our team was left alone to try thing because we were working so quickly. Once we realized isolation wasn't in our best interest we brought others in. By the time we pitched our design for the upcoming prioritization everyone had already bought in.
