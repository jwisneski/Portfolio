Working on behalf of the customer

In 2017 Amazon's one-click patent expired. We theorized more and more retailers would be using this to reduce the distance for customers to buy products. The biggest blocker for a one-click checkout at Kohl's is the use of discounts.

With my experience on previous discount projects, I took the lead on this one. We kicked off a series of bi-weekly tests to learn as much as we could about our customers and get quick feedback.

Today, the first phase of The Savings Launcher is live at Kohl's and it has resulted in sales of over $100 million in the first year.

The Kohl's discount model is complex

For the years leading up to this project the company stood by the idea that customers like the gamification of offers and they'll do work to get the best deal.

We challenged that assumption, first by targeting new customers and then by talking to the most loyal customers. The more we learned the more refined The Savings Launcher.

Inviting use

From the first interaction we aimed to make our experience fun. A less serious product, we thought, might lead to more open feedback. All 26 customer were unsure what a Savings Launcher is but all 26 wanted to find out, even without us directing them to it.

We were driven by the philosophy that conversion is more useful than margin so we gave the customer the best possible deal with no friction.

It started with a sketch

The project started with a single sketch. We identified some behaviors that our customers and our business partners wanted to emphasize. Each of these settings corresponded to one of these behaviors.

The patterns that were established in that first sketch allowed for extra information, alerts and a way to engage in saving without doing math.

The saving settings

We converted the sketch directly into a wireframe and used InVision to create a working prototype. Much of the rocket branding was left intact to keep the project fun for customers and the team.

In the spirit of our new branding, we used the term "Autopilot". This allowed new customers to get the best deal with one click

This prototype was tested in Milwaukee, WI and San Francisco, CA. We wanted to explore the concept, iterate quickly and continue to refine the product with subsequent tests.

Rapid iterative testing

We changed smaller parts of the prototype, like wording, and made larger changes as well. Throughout the testing process we overhauled the entire flow. This is where we first developed a pattern for talking about offers in a consistent way.

Showing the offers, we thought, would provide enough transparency for the customers to know which offers they were applying and feel good about their deal.

Refining the prototype

Through testing we were able to refine the prototype and simplify. Again, the customer does no work but is still able to get the best deal. For loyal customers we offered the option to tweak discounts.

Onboarding

Customers were having trouble understanding what each of these options mean so we started to develop an onboarding process. This also forced the team to look at why we needed an onboarding process.

The second version of the onboarding broke up the steps and explained them to the customer. We knew we could simplify the process so we kept working.

Ultimately this created too much friction and too many steps. We ended up discarding it in favor of an approach that fit our now evolving philosophy that the customer does no work.

The new onboarding process

Rather than pause our customer's exploration to show them what The Savings Launcher is, we let them explore. This proved to be a safe, easy way to test out different discounts. Customers can't use the wrong discounts and there are no error messages. The tool molds to the way customers want to interact with it.

Autopilot

Early in the design process we realized there needed to be an "easy mode". To keep with the nature of the product to this point, we labeled it "Autopilot". When engaged, it uses all of the possible discounts and gives the customer the best deal.

All 26 customers engaged Autopilot and compared the on and off states. 25 of those customers kept Autopilot on and continued to the checkout process

Multiple feedback mechanisms

We were very deliberate with how we showed the customer's discounts both with the stacking verbiage and with the functional animations we used.

Promoting the Kohl's Charge

As an added bonus for our business partners we wanted to think about driving adoption of the Kohl’s Charge Card. Customers with the card are far more likely to convert and convert more often.

When a customer chooses to use a Kohl’s Charge offer, they’re presented with the option to enter an existing card number or apply for a Kohl’s Charge.

Ultimately we needed to figure out how this would work with customer accounts but our efforts were focused on discounts as a whole.

“I feel like Kohl's has my back.”

Trust

It was at this point that we realized the most important insight about our customers. They expect Kohl's to act in their best interest because of their loyalty. Any business-centric thoughts of diluting Autopilot, withholding offers to conserve margins, were immediately halted. This would break down the trust that so many Kohl's customers have.

A transparent process

Throughout the process, we changed how we talked about offers with customers. We went back and forth between providing a glimpse into the Kohl’s Discount model, or educating, and letting our systems do the work for the customer.

We ended up with a pattern for all discounts that offers savings with the flick of a switch. Offers that can’t be combined will collapse so they can’t be used. No error messages required.

The anatomy of an offer

Each discount has the same basic information in common. In the marketing for offers these aren't always explained in the same way. We wanted to break from that tradition and add a layer of consistency to our offers.

The flow before

When we started this project customers had multiple options for adding discounts. Customers would manually type in codes and adding discounts would be trial and error.

The flow after

Our work vastly simplified the flow, both for our customers and our company.

Phase 1

The first phase of the project was released in April of 2017. While not all of the idea made it, we did make some big strides. Customers can enjoy a new level of transparency that has never existed at Kohl's.

Connecting customers and products

This was the first step in creating a new, more competitive Kohl's. We started to work backwards from checkout and refine what a product page should be. I'm looking forward to seeing some of the work we started in the coming months and years.

Reflections on The Savings Launcher

This project was so much fun. Not only was it a chance to think broadly but we were really designing with customers, not just for them.

I appreciate that this was one of the more pure design processes. Largely our team was left alone to try thing because we were working so quickly. Once we realized isolation wasn't in our best interest we brought others in. By the time we pitched our design for the upcoming prioritization everyone had already bought in.
