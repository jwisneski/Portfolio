Starting out at Nationwide

I came to Nationwide to release the app. The team needed an interaction Designer that could work quickly, take initiative and work colaboratively with business and development partners.

Thinking big

The app started as a completely unconstrained blue-sky prototype. This design was based on the customer, not the business and it was a huge departure from the way Nationwide's products were made up to that point.

Building small

To make the app scalable, design patterns were a must. What we could do immediately and what we planned to do long term were two vastly different things so we had to account for the app growing over time.

Navigating between spaces

We broke down lines of business in favor of organizing content how our customers view it. Policies show a customer's relationship with Nationwide. Customers can handle their money within Bills and view their open claims in Claims.

The most used features

We had to get the basics right. Every customer should be able to log in, pay a bill, file a claim and view their ID cards. If we couldn't do these things we couldn't release the app.

The first feature I led, before leading the entire app project, was the ID card feature. For some customers, this is the reason to download the app and sometimes the only reason to use it.

Built with design patterns

Some customers only open the app every 6 months so we wanted looked to common patterns on larger or more popular platforms. The ID card menu was heavily inspired by the Apple and Google wallets.

Testing with real customers

Our research teams were swamped with long-term projects. Most of the time the minimum turnaround on a usability test was 4 - 6 weeks. So we took the research into our own hands with guerilla testing sessions.

Taking on a lead role

Just a few months into my role at Nationwide, I took on the role as UX Lead for the app. Due to circumstance this happened quite literally overnight.

Now I was part Interaction Designer, part Project Manager and a project lead. I had to manage budgets, timelines, team members and stakeholder expectations, all while getting the app out of the door and keeping the big picture intact.

I was leading a team of specialists, not generalists. That meant understanding, and helping everyone else understand, each of the disciplines enough to move the conversation forward.

Pushed in the deep end

Soon after I took over our team lost a Copywriter to the lack of a contract renewal. Adding onto the stress, our business and development team was split into 6 business and development teams.

At the end of a string of bad luck I was forced to think about how we were working. My reflection led me to re-think 3 key things.

1) How we were interacting with our stakeholders

2) How we were working on behalf of the customer

3) How we kept the big picture in mind

So I approached them like a series of design problems.

Evangelizing design

The project changed the way Nationwide approached new projects. If my team was going to keep the quality up we needed to change the way Nationwide makes decisions and prioritizes features.

This meant we didn't just have to collaborate with partners but educate them on our process. We had to show them why it takes time to design things, how we think through problems and what we deliver.

I wanted to do this in a way that was respectful but got the point across. One of our team members suggested a series of quick talks inspired by TED.

Diagraming customer friction

I found myself retreading the same conversations over and over. It's hard to see the big picture when everyone is looking at a single screen. This leads to overlooking how seemingly small points of friction add up for customers.

I realized I could help resolve this by using some of our design artifacts to my advantage. Flows became far cleaner, easier to navigate and more spacious so I could walk through them while telling the story of the customer's journey. To highlight the places where friction adds up I used simple colors.

This was a simple solution that conveyed meaning beyond the way we use utilitarian flows so much of the time.

Managing timelines

We couldn't cover every team all the team. What I had to do was anticipate and work with Scrum Masters to place the right team members on the right teams at the right times.

Moving forward

After our big launch in January we have about 10 upcoming features, most are planned for 2018 and the rest are planned for 2019.

Listening to customers

We get feedback from our customers everyday and we keep an eye on our analytics to understand what our customers actually want. The projects we've prioritized are there because of this feedback.

Payments, repairs, coverages and more

Each new project is an opportunity to improve the experience for our customers not just add features that exist on our website. We try to anticipate the new projects so we have time to dig in to the important questions and understand customer expectations.

Reflections on The Nationwide App

This was a long project, not just the timeline but emotionally. Working with 6 teams, understanding UX as an agile process, launching an app and leading a team are a challenge individually. I did all of them at the same time.

I'm happy with what we built but as always I wish I started with what I know now. A year in and I'm still learning about teams that exist on the fringe of our work.

The app will continue to exist in the framework that we've built and it'll expand for several years. We created a great start for the app and the workflow but there's plenty of work left to do. Change takes time at a company this large. It's not just a design sprint, it's a design marathon.
