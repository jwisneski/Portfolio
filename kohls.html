The Mini Bag

Spring 2016 • In-House Experience Design

Reducing steps to check out by showing a shopping bag where ever the customer happens to be.

It’s dangerous to go alone, take this

  • A bag that is always with you
  • 6 month project
  • I was the design lead

Why?

To the most loyal Kohl’s customers, this is the shopping bag. The mini bag is a way to make quick changes and reduce the distance to checkout where conversion drops significantly.

The mini bag before the project

  • No pricing was shown just photos
  • Products couldn’t be changed or removed from the mini bag
  • Clicking a photo takes the customer to the Product Description Page
  • Full page ads (pictured) were, and are, common

What we did

What was off limits

  • Height of the bag couldn’t change by even a pixel
  • Order summary was out of scope
  • Kohl’s Cash and Yes2You rewards meters were part of a separate project and were left alone

We had to show price, quantity, size and color



So we had a problem. How do we fit all of this information into such a tiny space with so much visual noise around it?

We considered several blue-sky designs but ultimately our partners were more comfortable enhancing the current feature set.

Customers complaints around the Edit Shopping Bag button were common. Our hypothesis was a more prominent button would mean an increase in shopping bag views but that was proven wrong.

This was our first major insight and became a huge driver for the project. To the most loyal Kohl’s shopper this mini bag is the shopping bag.

Voice of the customer

Usability testing

With the card design in place, most of the products worked really well in the model. The remainder was a series of edge cases. BOGO, Buy One Get One has been around since the earliest days of retail but it broke that design.

We tried to find ways for the system to handle all the heavy lifting and even make it easier to get great deals on products but the most important aspect was that the system order the products in a logical way. That took a few rounds of testing with customers to figure out.

“I shouldn’t need a PhD in Kohl’s Math to check out”


  • We put this design in front of 12 customers in 2 rounds of tests
  • tasks were centered around price confidence and modifying size, color, quantity

We made it easier for a customer to see her price but we didn’t make it easier for her to understand that price. This laid the foundation for work we did during the Summer and Fall of 2015.
